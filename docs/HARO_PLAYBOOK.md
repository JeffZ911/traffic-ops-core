# HARO / Digital-PR Playbook — Quvii pilot

Earned editorial links (answering journalist source requests) are the safest,
cheapest, highest-ROI authority play for a zero-authority new site. Note: the
original **HARO** platform (Cision) shut down end-2024 — these are the live
successors. "HARO" below is shorthand for the journalist-request model.

Pilot site: **quvii.com** (home security). Expand to the other sites only
after this shows movement in the bi-weekly indexing census.

## Daily SOP (~15 min)
```
1. Open Featured.com + Qwoted (signed in as jeff@quvii.com).
2. Filter: home security / smart home / privacy / consumer tech.
3. Answer 1-2 queries you can genuinely speak to (match a template below).
4. Reply format:
   - First sentence answers the journalist's EXACT question (no preamble).
   - One specific, quotable point or small data nugget.
   - 80-130 words, plain text. No link in the body.
   - End with the attribution block.
5. Log every pitch in the ledger (data/haro_ledger_template.csv → your sheet).
6. Weekly: check which pitches got published; record the live link.
```

### Hit-rate rules
- **Reply within ~30 min** of a request — far higher hit rate than next-day.
- **Never sell Quvii** — give the journalist usable expertise; the link rides
  on the attribution.
- Same name / email / bio everywhere → journalists come back to you.
- If published **without** a link, email politely: *"Thanks for including me —
  would you mind crediting Quvii (quvii.com) as the source?"* — often works.

### Expectations
Slow burn: 1-2 pitches/day, **0-2 placements/week at first** is normal. Give it
a month. The bi-weekly indexing census quantifies whether Quvii's coverage /
impressions move.

## Answer templates (customize first 2 lines, keep the rest)

**① Home/smart-home security tips**
> The biggest mistake I see isn't the gear — it's setup. Three things matter
> more than which brand you buy: (1) put a camera at eye-level covering the
> approach to your main door, not just the door itself, so you capture a face
> before someone's on the porch; (2) turn on two-factor authentication on the
> app — an unprotected account is the real "hack," not the camera; and (3)
> decide up front whether you want local storage or a cloud subscription,
> because that one choice drives most of your long-term cost.

**② Are wireless/security cameras safe from hackers? / privacy**
> Most "hacked camera" stories aren't the camera being broken into — they're
> reused passwords and accounts with no two-factor enabled. The practical
> checklist: a unique password plus 2FA, firmware on auto-update, and cameras
> that offer end-to-end encryption and local storage so your footage isn't
> sitting on a third-party server longer than it needs to. If a camera has no
> clear data-retention or encryption policy, that tells you more about the
> company than any spec sheet does.

**③ How to prevent package / porch theft**
> Cameras catch thieves; they rarely stop them. What actually reduces porch
> theft is removing the opportunity: route deliveries to a locker or pickup
> point during peak season, require a signature on high-value orders, and pair
> a visible camera with a motion light and a two-way-audio doorbell — being
> spoken to in real time is a stronger deterrent than a sticker. If you use a
> camera, aim it at the street approach, not just the package, so the footage
> is actually useful to police.

**④ Are camera subscriptions worth it? / hidden costs**
> The sticker price of a camera is rarely the real price. Many models are
> nearly useless without a cloud plan — no recorded clips, just live view — so
> a $40 camera quietly becomes $40 plus $5-$10 a month, per camera, forever.
> Before buying, ask one question: what do I lose without the subscription? If
> the answer is "all recording," budget for it or buy a model that records
> locally to an SD card or hub. Over three years the subscription often costs
> more than the hardware.

**⑤ Biggest camera-buying mistakes**
> People buy on resolution and ignore what decides whether the camera works
> when it matters: how it powers (a battery cam you forget to charge is a dead
> cam — wired or PoE is more reliable), whether it records locally or only to
> the cloud, and how it handles night and weather. 4K sounds great but eats
> storage and bandwidth; for most homes a well-placed 1080p/2K camera with
> solid night vision and local storage beats a 4K camera streaming everything
> to a server you pay monthly to access.
